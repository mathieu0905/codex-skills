# Article Logic Audit Checklists

Use this reference when a text is long, logically dense, academic, technical, or locally fluent but globally incoherent.

## Reader-State Questions

Ask these questions while reading from front to back:

- What does the reader know at this exact point?
- What concept, claim, or goal does this sentence assume?
- Has that prerequisite already been introduced, or is the text borrowing clarity from a later section?
- What question does this paragraph create in the reader's mind?
- Is that question answered soon enough to keep trust?
- Does this paragraph make the next paragraph feel necessary?

## Paragraph Job Types

Assign each paragraph one primary job:

- motivation: explain why the topic matters;
- context: give background or prior work;
- gap: identify what is missing or unresolved;
- thesis: state the article's main claim;
- roadmap: preview the order of the argument;
- definition: introduce a term, scope, or distinction;
- method: explain what was done;
- evidence: present data, examples, analysis, or reasoning;
- interpretation: explain what the evidence means;
- limitation: narrow the claim or name a weakness;
- implication: explain consequences;
- transition: connect two argument stages;
- conclusion: consolidate the article's answer.

If a paragraph has many jobs, split it or make its hierarchy explicit. If it has no clear job, remove or rewrite it.

## Concept Order Audit

For each important term or construct, record:

- first mention;
- first definition;
- required prerequisites;
- whether the term changes meaning later;
- whether synonyms should be unified;
- whether the concept appears in a figure, table, method, limitation, or conclusion before it is explained.

Common failures:

- name-before-need: a term appears before the reader knows why it matters;
- use-before-definition: the prose relies on an undefined term;
- definition-after-argument: the key distinction arrives after the claim that needed it;
- concept pileup: several new terms appear in one paragraph without hierarchy;
- silent renaming: one concept is renamed later without warning.

## Claim-Evidence Audit

For each important claim, classify support:

- supported: direct evidence or reasoning is present;
- partially supported: evidence exists but scope is narrower than the claim;
- unsupported: assertion without evidence;
- contradicted: later text conflicts with it;
- misplaced: support appears too late or before the claim is intelligible.

Check for scope drift:

- "can" becoming "does";
- "in this setting" becoming "generally";
- "suggests" becoming "proves";
- method feasibility becoming empirical effectiveness;
- one example becoming a category-level conclusion.

## Section Correspondence Audit

Compare article parts in pairs:

- title vs. actual scope;
- abstract vs. body;
- introduction promises vs. section content;
- research questions vs. method and evidence;
- method claims vs. results;
- results vs. discussion;
- limitations vs. method and evaluation;
- conclusion vs. demonstrated contribution.

When a mismatch appears, decide whether to revise the promise, add missing support, move material, or narrow the claim.

## Method-Limitation Audit

Build a table with these columns:

- method or design choice;
- problem it is supposed to address;
- evidence that it works;
- limitation that remains;
- whether the limitation is acknowledged in the right place;
- required revision.

Common failures:

- limitation appears only at the end but should constrain earlier claims;
- method section promises coverage that results never evaluate;
- limitation names a weakness that the method supposedly solved;
- future work introduces a major missing component that should have been framed as out of scope earlier;
- the discussion treats an untested method assumption as established.

## Transition Audit

A good transition states the real relationship between ideas. Identify the relationship before adding connective prose:

- cause: A leads to B;
- contrast: B differs from A in a relevant way;
- narrowing: B is a specific case of A;
- expansion: B adds another dimension to A;
- consequence: B follows if A is accepted;
- concession: A is true, but B limits it;
- sequence: B must come after A for the reader to understand.

Avoid decorative transitions that hide a missing inference. If the relationship cannot be named, the order or argument may be wrong.

## Revision Moves

Use the smallest move that solves the global issue:

- add a signpost when the structure is sound but the reader needs orientation;
- add a bridge sentence when an inference is missing;
- move a definition earlier when a later concept is needed sooner;
- split a paragraph when jobs compete;
- merge paragraphs when they repeat the same job;
- reorder sections when prerequisites are inverted;
- narrow a claim when support is weaker than the wording;
- add evidence when the claim is central and support is missing;
- remove a claim when it creates an unpaid promise outside the article's scope.
